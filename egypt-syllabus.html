Ancient Egypt

The Long Timeline

c. 6000 BCE – 30 BCE
c. 6000 – 3100 BCE
Predynastic Egypt
Farming villages form along the Nile. Naqada cultures develop pottery, copper tools, and the first hieroglyph-like symbols. Upper and Lower Egypt remain separate.
c. 3100 BCE
Narmer Unifies Egypt
King Narmer (Menes) joins Upper and Lower Egypt, founding the First Dynasty. The Narmer Palette commemorates the moment — our earliest "history book."
c. 2686 – 2181 BCE
Old Kingdom — The Pyramid Age
Djoser builds the first Step Pyramid. Khufu, Khafre, and Menkaure raise the Giza plateau. The pharaoh is a living god; the state runs on grain and labour.
c. 2181 – 2055 BCE
First Intermediate Period
Drought, weak central rule, and local warlords fracture the kingdom. "A man sees his son as his enemy," an Egyptian lament records.
c. 2055 – 1650 BCE
Middle Kingdom — The Classical Age
Mentuhotep II reunifies. A golden age of literature ("The Tale of Sinuhe"), portraiture, and Nubian expansion. Egypt becomes more introspective.
c. 1650 – 1550 BCE
Hyksos & the Second Intermediate
Asiatic rulers — the Hyksos — seize the Delta. They bring the horse and chariot; Egypt learns the tools it will soon use to strike back.
c. 1550 – 1069 BCE
New Kingdom — Egypt as Empire
Ahmose expels the Hyksos. Hatshepsut trades to Punt. Thutmose III conquers as far as the Euphrates. Amenhotep III presides over unmatched wealth.
c. 1353 – 1336 BCE
Akhenaten's Heresy
Amenhotep IV becomes Akhenaten, abolishes the old gods, and builds a new capital, Amarna, devoted to the sun-disc Aten. A brief, radical monotheism.
c. 1332 BCE
Tutankhamun Restores the Gods
A nine-year-old boy-king reverses Akhenaten's revolution. He dies at ~18 — and his small, intact tomb will stun the 20th century world.
1274 BCE
Ramses II at Kadesh
Ramses the Great battles the Hittites to a draw. The peace treaty that follows is the oldest surviving international treaty in history.
c. 1069 – 664 BCE
Third Intermediate Period
Egypt splinters. Libyan and then Nubian (Kushite) kings rule — the 25th "Black Pharaohs" dynasty revives old traditions and builds pyramids in Sudan.
525 BCE
Persian Conquest
Cambyses II adds Egypt to the Achaemenid Empire. Egyptians will spend the next two centuries under foreign rule, with brief independent interludes.
332 BCE
Alexander the Great Arrives
Welcomed as a liberator, Alexander founds Alexandria — destined to become the ancient world's greatest library and intellectual capital.
305 – 30 BCE
The Ptolemaic Dynasty
Alexander's general Ptolemy founds a Greek-speaking royal line. They rule as pharaohs, worship Egyptian gods, and never learn Egyptian — until Cleopatra.
30 BCE
Cleopatra's Fall
Defeated with Mark Antony at Actium, Cleopatra VII dies. Egypt becomes a Roman province. The 3,000-year pharaonic tradition ends.

8-Week Syllabus

~24 hours total
1
Foundations
The Nile & the First Egyptians
c. 6000 – 3100 BCE
Goal: Understand why Egypt happened — the geography, agriculture, and slow convergence that made a unified state possible.
  • The Nile's annual flood and why it was "predictable bounty"
  • Naqada cultures, Badarian pottery, and Predynastic society
  • Upper vs. Lower Egypt — two crowns, two lands
  • Narmer, Menes, and the unification (c. 3100 BCE)
  • What makes a civilisation? Writing, cities, hierarchy, monumental scale
Read / Watch Wilkinson Ch. 1–2 worldhistory.org/egypt Fletcher (first 30 min)
ReflectionIf the Nile flooded unpredictably like the Tigris, would Egypt have become one of the most stable ancient states? Defend your answer.
2
Old Kingdom
The Pyramid Age
c. 2686 – 2181 BCE
Goal: See how a god-king theology, surplus grain, and organised labour produced the most iconic monuments in history.
  • Djoser and Imhotep — the Step Pyramid at Saqqara
  • The Great Pyramid of Khufu — logistics, not magic
  • Khafre, Menkaure, and the Sphinx
  • The pharaoh as Horus on earth — ma'at as cosmic balance
  • How the Old Kingdom collapsed: climate, overreach, and decentralisation
Read / Watch Wilkinson Ch. 3–5 Brier Lectures 2–4 AEO: Pyramids section
ReflectionModern estimates say the Great Pyramid needed ~20,000 workers for ~20 years. What does that tell you about Old Kingdom state capacity?
3
Middle Kingdom
Collapse, Reunification & Classical Voice
c. 2181 – 1650 BCE
Goal: Watch Egypt fracture, heal, and find a new cultural voice — and see how Egyptians themselves remembered these times.
  • The First Intermediate Period — "nomarchs" (local lords) take over
  • Mentuhotep II's reunification and the Theban rise
  • Middle Kingdom literature: Sinuhe, Ipuwer, Instruction texts
  • Portraiture changes: worried, weathered pharaohs replace idealised faces
  • The Hyksos invasion — how foreign rule ended the Middle Kingdom
Read / Watch Wilkinson Ch. 6–8 Docuseries Ep. 2
ReflectionCompare a Middle Kingdom royal statue with an Old Kingdom one. What do the faces tell you about how kingship changed?
4
New Kingdom I
Empire — From Ahmose to Amenhotep III
c. 1550 – 1353 BCE
Goal: Understand how Egypt transformed from a river valley into the ancient world's first superpower.
  • Ahmose I drives out the Hyksos — the 18th Dynasty begins
  • Hatshepsut — female pharaoh, trader, temple-builder at Deir el-Bahri
  • Thutmose III's 17 campaigns — the "Napoleon of Egypt"
  • The Amarna Letters and Egypt's first diplomatic corps
  • Amenhotep III — luxury, Colossi of Memnon, the empire at its peak
Read / Watch Wilkinson Ch. 9–10 Brier Lectures 6–7
ReflectionHatshepsut ruled for ~22 prosperous years, yet her successor tried to erase her from monuments. Why was a successful woman pharaoh so threatening in retrospect?
5
New Kingdom II
Akhenaten, Tutankhamun & Ramesside Egypt
c. 1353 – 1069 BCE
Goal: Study a religious revolution, a counter-revolution, and the slow decline of imperial Egypt.
  • Akhenaten & Nefertiti — monotheism, Amarna art, the closed temples
  • Tutankhamun's restoration and Howard Carter's 1922 discovery
  • Ramses II — Kadesh, Abu Simbel, the Hittite peace treaty
  • Merneptah Stele — the earliest mention of "Israel"
  • Sea Peoples, internal strife, and why the New Kingdom ended
Read / Watch Wilkinson Ch. 11–13 Fletcher: Amarna segment AEO: Akhenaten & Tut
ReflectionWas Akhenaten a genuine visionary or a destructive autocrat? Marshal evidence for both views.
6
Culture
Gods, the Afterlife & Everyday Life
Thematic
Goal: Step off the political timeline and live inside the Egyptian worldview for a week.
  • The pantheon: Ra, Osiris, Isis, Horus, Anubis, Thoth, Ma'at
  • The weighing of the heart — the Book of the Dead
  • Mummification: 70 days, natron, and the canopic jars
  • Peasants, scribes, artisans — life at Deir el-Medina
  • Women in Egypt: rights, work, marriage, divorce
Read / Watch AEO: Gods & Religion, Daily Life worldhistory.org: Religion Docuseries: Religion ep.
ReflectionEgyptian women had property rights unusual for the ancient world. Why might riverine agriculture and temple economics have produced that outcome?
7
Expression
Writing, Art & Monument
Thematic
Goal: Learn to "read" Egyptian visual and written culture — the conventions that make it instantly recognisable.
  • Hieroglyphs, hieratic, and demotic — three scripts, one language
  • The Rosetta Stone and Champollion's 1822 breakthrough
  • Canonical art: frontal eye, side head, composite view — why?
  • Temple architecture: pylons, hypostyle halls, sanctuaries (Karnak, Luxor)
  • The Valley of the Kings — hidden tombs, robbed tombs, one intact
Read / Watch Wilkinson: Art & Writing chapters Brier: Hieroglyphs lecture worldhistory.org: Architecture
ReflectionEgyptian art barely changed for 3,000 years. Is that cultural rigidity — or a conscious choice to express eternity? Support your view.
8
Late & Ptolemaic
Decline, Foreign Rule & Legacy
c. 1069 BCE – 30 BCE
Goal: Trace Egypt's long twilight — and see why its legacy outlasted every empire that ever ruled it.
  • The Third Intermediate Period — Libyan, Nubian, and Assyrian overlords
  • The 25th Nubian ("Kushite") Dynasty — Black pharaohs revive tradition
  • Persian conquest (525 BCE), brief independence, reconquest
  • Alexander the Great and the founding of Alexandria (332 BCE)
  • The Ptolemies — Greek rulers, Egyptian gods, the Library & Lighthouse
  • Cleopatra VII, Caesar, Antony, Actium — and Roman Egypt
  • Coptic Egypt, Arab conquest, and how hieroglyphs fell silent
Read / Watch Wilkinson: final chapters Fletcher: Late & Ptolemaic Docuseries: Cleopatra ep.
CapstoneIn one page, argue: when did "Ancient Egypt" really end? At 30 BCE? At the closure of the last Isis temple in 537 CE? Or only when Champollion read the Rosetta Stone?
